The challenge

No outbound motion. A product that needed the right founders.

The client was an early-stage AI platform built to help founders navigate high-stakes decisions — hiring gaps, fundraising pivots, operational bottlenecks. Think of it as an always-on strategic thinking partner. The product had real depth, but zero structured outbound system.

The goal was clear: find founders who were already living the problems this product solves, warm them up without burning them, and get them into a real conversation with the founder. The entire funnel — from sourcing to booked call — needed to be built from scratch.

The process

Three-stage funnel: signal, warm-up, convert.

1

Signal-based lead sourcing with Phantombuster + Apollo + Clay

Rather than building a broad list from thin criteria, I reverse-engineered where high-intent founders were already showing up publicly. I identified LinkedIn posts from industry thought leaders discussing the exact pain points the product addressed: hiring gaps, fundraising challenges, operational strain, and the loneliness of founding.

Using Phantombuster, I scraped engagement data from 50+ targeted posts. Comments were treated as high-signal (the person is actively thinking about this problem); likes were lower signal and deprioritised. From an initial pool of 1,000+ profiles, I filtered down to ~215 qualified leads — founders who were not just present, but actively engaging with the right problems. Apollo.io and Clay were used to enrich the list with business context, recent activity, and contact data.

One key lesson: LinkedIn flagged my personal account during scraping. I adapted by reducing scrape volume and moving research activity off LinkedIn, reserving the platform only for direct outreach at a low, controlled cadence.

2

Warm-up via weekly live roundtable events

Cold outreach to cold leads rarely works with busy founders. Instead of going straight to a pitch, scraped leads were funnelled into a weekly live roundtable event hosted on LinkedIn Live. These events pulled in 40–70 attendees per week, with approximately 50% of attendees traceable back to the scraped list — confirmed by cross-referencing names from the enriched spreadsheet.

A weekly email newsletter ran in parallel via Brevo, achieving 10–15% open rates over the campaign period. The roundtable served a critical function: by the time outreach happened, these founders already had a positive association with the brand. Trust was built before the ask.

3

Hyper-personalized Slack DM conversion

After founders joined the community Slack post-event, I ran a direct outreach campaign to convert them into 1:1 demo calls with the founder. Early attempts using generic follow-ups — referencing past event attendance or vague product pitches — produced poor results. Busy founders simply didn't respond.

The breakthrough came from real-time research. Before each outreach, I identified a specific, recent development in that founder's world — a funding round, a new project launch, a public announcement, a challenge mentioned during the event — and opened with that signal directly. The outreach no longer felt like a mass campaign. It felt like someone had actually paid attention.

Result: Out of 15–20 founders personally reached out to via Slack, 10 booked calls with the founder. All 10 were onboarded to the app during that first call — the founder ran a live demo and converted them on the spot. Several later became vocal advocates, sharing their experience at subsequent roundtable events.

Real examples

What the personalization actually looked like.

Example A
Food & beverage founder — post-crowdfunding expansion
Converted → Active user

Signal identified: Founder had recently closed a crowdfunding round and was publicly signalling intentions to expand her business into a new market. The timing — capital raised, growth ambition unlocked — was a high-intent moment.

Why this angle: This was not a generic congratulations. It was a recognition that she was at a pivotal inflection point where a strategic thinking partner would have real value. The complimentary call was positioned as a gift at the right moment — not a sales pitch.

Outreach message (paraphrased)

"Hi [Name], congrats on your recent crowdfunding — that's a real milestone. I noticed you're working on expanding beyond your current market, and that's exactly the kind of pivotal moment our founder loves to think through with people. He'd love to offer you a complimentary call to explore how he could support what you're building right now."

She booked the call, received a demo, was offered a pilot, became an active user, and later spoke about the platform at the next founder roundtable.
Example B
Research institute founder — new project launch
Converted → Active user

Signal identified: Founder had recently launched a new research project and had mentioned during the roundtable event that she was navigating a difficult internal team situation. Two signals — public momentum and private pressure — created a clear picture of where she was.

Why this angle: The internal conflict was not referenced directly — that would have felt intrusive. Instead, it was used as context to understand her headspace. The outreach led with the positive signal (the project launch) while the underlying awareness of her situation informed the tone: empathetic, not pushy.

Outreach message (paraphrased)

"Hi [Name], I saw that you're ramping up your new research project — exciting timing. Given everything you're navigating right now, our founder thought it might be worth a quick conversation. He works with founders on exactly this kind of moment — when there's a lot moving at once and you want someone who can think alongside you."

Responded positively, booked the call, and became an active user of the platform.
Key learning

The single thing that moved the needle.

Specificity beats volume every time.

Generic messages referencing past event attendance or vague product benefits barely registered with busy founders. What converted was the moment the outreach showed that someone had actually paid attention — a recent business development, a public announcement, a challenge mentioned in passing. The research investment per lead was higher, but the quality of conversation that followed was incomparably better. People respond when they feel seen, not just targeted. That's the core principle behind every message in this campaign.
